About the project

Open/Opaque is a digital humanities project that investigates what open data on police use of force affords for our understanding of policing. In particular, this project is interested in whether police data helps researchers understand how and why the police use force. Open/Opaque uses visualization to explore a sampling of use of force datasets, concluding that limitations in the participation, scope, and consistency of the data mean that this data is more opaque than open.

About the data

In 2014, President Obama launched the Taskforce on 21st Century Policing in an effort to build trust between communities and police departments in the wake of the police murders of Michael Brown, Eric Garner, and Tamir Rice and the ensuing uprisings against police violence. On the recommendation of this task force, the Police Data Initiative was created to gather data from police departments around the country and make it publicly available as a means of increasing transparency and therefore trust. The Police Data Initiative's online repository includes datasets from 120 departments around the country.

Open/Opaque focuses on the use of force datasets available through the Police Data Initiative. Aside from officer involved shootings, this data represents one of the most controversial police actions. I decided to focus on use of force rather than officer involved shootings, since the latter has been the focus of other very thorough digital humanities projects, such as the Washington Post's police shooting database. Other projects such as Mapping Police Violence and Fatal Encounters, visualize police killings. There are not similarly high-profile projects that look at the much more mundane, but still serious and potentially fatal, force that officers exercise on a daily basis. And while resources on police killings can look to other sources to supplement incomplete or unavailable police data, non-fatal police use of force usually doesn't make it into the newspapers, making data collected and shared by the police all the more crucial to understanding how and why police use force.

Of the 17 datasets on police use of force available on the Police Data Initiative site, I selected those from larger police departments and with more complete data. For example, I decided not to work with the Seattle Police Department's data because it included so few data points for each incident. After creating visualizations for each of the selected datasets (see my section on methods below), I eliminated another dataset because the structure of the data was too different from the other datasets, and I couldn't create comparable visualizations with it. The remaining use of force datasets that are represented by this project are: Cincinnati Police Department, Dallas Police Department, Indianapolis Metropolitan Police Department, Portland Police Bureau.

About the methods

I used OpenRefine to clean my data, Tableau to visualize it, and Mobirise to construct this website.

In the case of the Dallas Police Department data, each year was represented by a separate document, so I first combined the data into one document following the instructions here (thanks to Professor Posner for the help!) My data cleaning was minimal, just enough to clean up any errors or inconsistencies. I also used OpenRefine to break out columns that contained multiple data points--for example, some of the datasets listed all subject demographics in one field--and to the parse date fields into a Tableau-friendly format.

Visualizing the data in Tableau helped me understand what insights the data offered. One of the biggest obstacles in doing this project was the inconsistency among the datasets (discussed more on the Findings page). This ended up being one of the biggest revelations of the project and one that I tried to show through comparison of similar visualizations with the different datasets. While I used consistent labeling in my visualizations to help viewers understand the correlation among the datasets, for the most part I did not edit the language used in the actual data. In some cases, I was not even able to translate the police-jargon for myself. My hope is that, where viewers encounter opacity in the data, it will show the underlying flaws in the data itself and not in my visualizations of it!
